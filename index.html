Our Vision

Manufacturing processes have limitations that cause deviations and deformities, thus require inspections. Current surface finishes inspections rely on custom processes with expensive tools or human labor.

We aim to fix that with inspeksi, the smart defect detector. Our plug and play solution offers a wide range of automated features to make inspection of newly manufactured parts fast, efficient, and effective. Simply insert your part into the enclosure and watch how the automated system scans your part and detects defects using a sophisticated AI model that only increases in accuracy as you continue to use the product.

Enclosure Redesign

In order to source the material from the Machine Shop, the availability had to be considered. The original 1/2'’ angle brackets had to be changed to the 3/4'’ angle brackets. This required a change to the hole positions. In addition, space for the nut had to be verified. In the original design, there was a 1/16’’ gap between the frame and the panels in some locations. To resolve this, a notch was made in the angle bracket to create a closed seam at the enclosure edges. The final cost of the enclosure including material, machining and tax is $208.49. Some additional work was done to clean rough edges.

Instance Segmentation

In the previous term, an object detection algorithm called the Single-shot Multibox Detector (SSD) (Liu et al., 2016) was used to classify and localize defects in an image. The algorithm however has a limitation since it can only localizes defects in terms of bounding boxes, and thus is not able to quantify the size of the defects perfectly. An example of the output from object detection model is shown in the figure below.

In contrast, instance segmentation algorithms, like the Mask R-CNN (He et al., 2017), are designed to localize object in an image on the pixel level. This is definitely desirable for the application of detecting defects, since one of the metrics to quantify defects are by measuring the size of the defects. In order to test the algorithm, a Mask R-CNN model was trained on a small dataset that contains images with scratches. Indeed, the algorithm was able to classify and localize the scratches in the image better than the object detection model. The preliminary results of using Mask R-CNN can be found below.

Establishing Communication with Dynamixel Smart Serial Servos

Communicating with the smart servos is a crucial stage in development to initiate the controls software development for moving the robotic arm in a confined space. However, since two different types of servos are being used (which use two different voltage levels), two Robotis developed controllers were purchased to allow us to operate at the distinct voltage levels of our motor types. The electronics were assembled to establish communication with the XL series motor this week - using a variable buck converter to reduce a fixed 12V source to 7.4V, the level needed for the XL series motor. Currently, we are able to communciate to the motors from a laptop->controller->motor using serial communication. The next goal moving forward is to utilise a RasberryPi to communicate to the controller using UART or USB (still under investigation). In parallel, electronics assembly for the AX series motors will be completed this week to allow for full communication with all motors. In addition, this week, backdrivability tests will be conducted to see how to read status information from the motors.

Robotic Arm Redesigns

The arm was assembled from the shoulder to the end effector, during this process mistakes in the original design were marked down and used to inform a redesign. The issues mostly consisted of interference, wrong hole measurements and a lack of counterbores for the bolt heads. The arm has 5 plastic parts that make up the frame, the base plate, the turntable, the humerus, the radius and the camera mount. Each part is connected to the next part through one of the four motors and fastened using bolt and nuts. The base plate lacked counterbore holes, without them the turntable would interfere during its rotation. The new base plate included the counterbore holes. The second version of the base plate also include a space for the bolts to lower the motor and give room for the wires to pass through. The turntable’s design had a few flaws, the fillets facing motor 1 interfered with the motor’s motion reducing the range of motion from 180 degrees to 15 degrees. The second version of the turntable has a much slimmer frame without fillets facing the motors allowing the full range of motion. When fully assembled the humerus wasn’t securely held at the shoulder joint allowing it to shake significantly during trial operations. The bolt heads on elbow side interfered with the radius. To address those issues, a hook like protrusion was added to the shoulder end of the humerus allowing the weight of the arm to be supported on both sides during operation. Counterbores were added to the holes on the wrist. The radius’ elbow cutout restricted the range of motion of the arm. To counteract this the cutout was lengthened. The arm when assembled, lacking the camera and the wiring, weighed in at 0.9lbs which meant the addition X grams of from the cameras and wiring would take it over the X weight limit. To reduce the weight the heaviest pieces, the humerus and the radius were lightened. By introducing a cutout in the middle of each piece X lbs was removed.

Completing Electrical Assembly of Robotic Arm

The robotic arm uses 2 types of motors, operating at 11.1V and 7.4V. Consequently, since other devices in the project operate at 5V, the team agreed to use a 12V supply and use a buck converter to create three voltage rails (11.1V, 7.4V, and 5V). After connecting and tuning the various buck converters, the team tested if all motors were able to connect to the their respective controller. Since Dynamixel motors use different protocol (USB2DYNAMIXEL), a unique, half-duplex capable controller that is compatible for both types of motors was used to communicate with the serial servos. In summary, 3 buck converters were used, 2 Dynamixel controllers were used, and a 12V, 100W power supply that is CSA approved.
